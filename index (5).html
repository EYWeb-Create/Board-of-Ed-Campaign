About Aaron

As an AU Park resident and a DC public school parent, I've seen firsthand how our community is served by our public schools. I've built my career around that same sense of service. Professionally, I've worked in government, research, and advocacy to expand healthcare access, including helping to build the Health Insurance Marketplace during implementation of the Affordable Care Act. My commitment to service began even earlier in Mali, West Africa, where I served as a Community Health Peace Corps Volunteer and met my wife, Meg. There I did everything from dig wells to organize an annual HIV-awareness basketball tournament and teach in a local high school.

These experiences shaped my belief that public institutions are at their best when they are accessible, accountable, and rooted in the community. As the son of a retired public school teacher and a product of public education, I view strong neighborhood schools as more than just buildings: they are often the heart of our communities.

Today, I am the father of two DC natives attending public schools in Ward 3. My belief in the power of public education has driven me to be an engaged and active parent. I have served on the Local School Advisory Team (LSAT) at Alice Deal Middle School, served as the treasurer on the board of our elementary school's parent-run aftercare and enrichment program, and volunteered and coached my kids' sports teams.

During our time in DC schools, our children have been taught by extremely talented, deeply committed educators. However, we have also encountered challenges that required us to advocate fiercely for our kids. We've seen the DC Public Schools (DCPS) administration fall short when it comes to accountability and responding to parent concerns or even keeping parents informed. Those experiences are why I'm running to be the Ward 3 representative to the DC State Board of Education. I believe we deserve a representative who listens to the community, speaks up for them, and consistently advocates for excellence and accountability for every public school in DC.

How Are Our Public Schools Managed and What Is the DC State Board of Education?

In 2007, DC shifted to mayoral control of schools to address decades of challenges with school administration and underperformance. Under this model, the Mayor has direct responsibility for the school system and appoints the leaders of the major education agencies. This includes the Chancellor of DCPS and the State Superintendent, who handles federal funding and citywide standards for all public schools, including public charter schools. The Deputy Mayor for Education coordinates across these different agencies, while other agencies like the Department of Health manage specific services, like school nursing.

The DC City Council also plays a major role through its power over the city budget and its ability to pass new laws. While the Mayor manages the day-to-day operations, the Council holds oversight hearings to check on the performance of education agencies and must approve the funding that schools receive each year. This gives the Council a level of authority over the system's finances and legal framework that other bodies do not have. However, the Council has no standing education committee, with education issues going through the Committee of the Whole.

The State Board of Education is the only elected part of this system specifically dedicated to education, but its actual power is extremely limited. Board members are part-time and rely on limited board staff. While the Board approves certain high-level rules like graduation requirements and academic standards, it does not have the authority to set school budgets, choose specific curricula, or make education leadership staffing decisions. Because the Mayor controls the funding and the leadership of the school system, the Board serves as a voice for community members rather than a body with management power. In the larger DC education system, the Board in its current form acts as a bridge between the community and city officials, even though the Mayor's office has the final word on how schools are operated day-to-day.

Even though the Board lacks much formal authority, it can still be an important platform. Board members are in a unique position to be a voice for community members, raising concerns and questions to school leaders, and to make sure community members are aware of what's happening across the school system. Being able to accomplish both those priorities depends heavily on being able to establish and maintain strong relationships with staff in the Deputy Mayor's office, OSSE, and DCPS; City Councilmembers; other Board members; school leaders; educators; and parents.

What I Stand For

Parents and families deserve a representative who will advocate for accountability, high standards, and a genuine voice in their children's education.

Accountability

Parents and families need accountability from our schools and the city power structures that oversee public education in DC. That means transparency in decision-making, responsiveness to concerns, clear communication about what's happening in our kids' classrooms before changes are made, and the ability to provide meaningful feedback that informs decision-making. It also means ensuring that DC schools fulfill their obligation to provide all students, especially those with special needs, the services and supports to which they are entitled. I will advocate for the Council to establish a standing education committee and to expand oversight authority of schools to the State Board of Education, along with the resources necessary to conduct that oversight.

High Academic Standards

We can't shortcut our children's education. We need to pursue innovation and new approaches while recognizing that some fundamentals of learning work. As part of this, strong DCPS neighborhood schools must remain the cornerstones of our communities. Across all public schools in DC, I support kids reading full books, mastering core subjects, and being challenged to reach their full potential.

A Voice for Communities

In addition to having a venue to advocate for their own children, families deserve someone who listens to them, speaks up for their children and schools, and communicates back to them about what's happening in the broader DC education community. That means having a Ward 3 representative committed to building and maintaining productive relationships with LSATs, PTAs/PTOs, parents, teachers, administrators, and other elected officials around the city. I will send regular Ward 3 newsletters highlighting what is happening on the Board, as well as what's happening in schools in Ward 3 and across the city. I will also meet regularly with parents and community members at Ward 3 events and in virtual and in-person office hours.

Education Technology, Screen Time, and AI

DC took a necessary first step when it banned personal devices in schools. Despite that, families have noticed an explosion in the use of educational technology and screen time both in school and at home for schoolwork. Educational technology isn't inherently bad, as long as it's used strategically and intentionally as a tool to supplement teaching and is evaluated for effectiveness rigorously before and after adoption. Similarly, AI must be treated as a tool that can free up time for teachers and supplement student learning, while not undermining their development of basic skills, like reading, writing, and math. I support assessing how much screen time students are getting in school or at home for schoolwork; regularly evaluating whether educational websites, applications, and tools are effective; and ensuring students aren't inadvertently being exposed to inappropriate or non-educational content in school or on school devices. I also support establishing a dedicated AI specialist role within the State Board of Education to develop a policy and training framework for AI integration in DC schools that prioritizes student safety, data privacy, academic integrity, and AI literacy for students, teachers, and parents. In all of this, families need to have a meaningful say in how educational technology and AI are used in schools and by students.

School Health & Safety

I believe in having one nurse per school who knows the students and community. The District has moved away from that to a "cluster" model that puts a nurse in the position of overseeing the health of students across multiple schools, in theory supervising health technicians who are on-site. In reality, the system has resulted in poor nursing retention and inadequate staffing of both nurses and health technicians. School nurses provide a critical safety net for all students by managing everyday health needs and acute injuries. We need a system that ensures students are safe and focused on learning, particularly those with specific medical requirements such as severe allergies or those who need assistance with medication. I support returning to one full-time nurse in every school. Until that is achieved, I support refining the cluster model, with the input of nurses, so that it ensures consistent access to highly trained professionals, provides appropriate clinical supervision, and operates in a manner designed to attract and retain experienced nursing staff.

Why Now?

The way our children learn is changing rapidly. From the influence of new technologies to shifting approaches in the classroom, our students are at a critical pivot point. Our community needs an advocate at the table to ensure parent, family, and teacher voices are heard.

That advocacy is particularly urgent because DC is on the cusp of a historic transition. With two new At-Large Councilmembers, our first new Congressional Delegate since 1991, and a new Mayor for the first time in 12 years, our city government is about to undergo a massive shift. This change in leadership will likely bring a cascade of other changes in school administration, representing a generational opportunity to reset our expectations. Now is the time to demand that city leaders prioritize high standards, intentional use of technology, and genuine community and family engagement in our public schools.

It will be critical to have a parent and community advocate representing Ward 3 on the State Board of Education.
