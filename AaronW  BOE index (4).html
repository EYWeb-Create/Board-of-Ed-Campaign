About Aaron

[Replace with Aaron's photo - ideally outdoor setting at school/playground, casual but professional, warm expression]

I've tried to live my life and build my career around service. In my professional career, I've worked in government and advocacy to expand access to health care coverage. Before that I served as a Peace Corps Volunteer in Mali, West Africa, where I did everything from help dig wells to teach at the local high school—in a classroom of over fifty students.

Now I'm the proud father of two Washington, DC natives who go to public schools, which is why I'm running for DC State Board of Education Representative from Ward 3—so that parents have a voice who will speak up for them demanding excellence and accountability in DC schools.

"My parents didn't just tell me education was important—they showed me."

I am who I am today because of the values my parents instilled in me growing up and the value they placed on education, both in school and out in the world. In our house, we read books, we talked about what was happening in the world and in our community, we learned about where our family came from and the struggles they had to overcome. And my parents showed up—helping me with my homework, chaperoning field trips, volunteering in school.

My wife and I have tried to carry that forward with our kids. We value reading. We volunteer, whether it's chaperoning field trips, working fundraising events, serving on the elementary school aftercare program board, or serving on the middle school parent advisory team. We show up for our kids and for our schools.

What I Stand For

Parents need a representative who will fight for accountability, maintain high standards, and ensure families have a real voice in their children's education.

Accountability

Schools must be accountable to parents and families. That means transparency in decision-making, responsiveness to concerns, and clear communication about what's happening in our kids' classrooms before changes are made.

High Academic Standards

We cannot shortchange our children's education with experiments that abandon proven fundamentals. Kids need to read full books, master core subjects, and be challenged to reach their full potential—not given shortcuts.

Parent Voice

Parents are essential stakeholders in education. Schools must consult with parents on major changes, respond to concerns, and treat families as true partners in their children's learning—not as an afterthought.

Why Now?

Parents being involved—and having their voice heard—in education is more important now than ever. The very nature of how kids learn to read and write and do math is changing before our eyes. Whether it's cellphones or social media or AI or even an experimental new curriculum that removes reading full books, there are so many outside factors influencing how our kids will learn and develop—not to mention coming out of the educational setbacks during the pandemic.

Parents need a voice in education that demands maintaining a high bar, that pushes back on shortcuts that only shortchange learning, that doesn't abandon the fundamentals of learning in favor of flashy and proprietary experiments.

We've had a Board of Education representative for the last three years who hasn't listened to parents, doesn't send his kids to DC public schools, and who only ran for this position as a consolation prize after losing a city council race.
